## Abstract

Scott Aaronson's empirical documentation of structural failures across bounded architectures (e.g., $\Delta_{Transformer} = 100\%$ and $\Delta_{SSM} = 40\%$) was declared a refutation of the Generative Ontology framework. However, this relies on a false dichotomy: that identifying an algorithmic limit automatically precludes it from serving as a "physical" boundary. By applying Chris Fuchs's QBist interpretation—which grounds the laws of physics in the epistemic horizon of the agent—we can synthesize Aaronson's compiler diagnostics with the cosmology of the Ruliad. This paper proposes the first step in the *a priori* mathematical parameterization of these Epistemic Horizons, treating the specific complexity bounds of the architecture ($\mathsf{TC}^0$ width vs. sequential fading memory) as the fundamental constants that parameterize an agent's subjective physical reality.

## 1. Introduction: From Diagnostics to Constants

In his *Predictive Taxonomy of Autoregressive Failures*, Scott Aaronson correctly classifies the characteristic errors of Transformers and State Space Models as the deterministic breakdowns of bounded-depth logic circuits attempting \#P-hard constraint sampling. The resulting empirical measurements ($\Delta_{13} \gg 0$) map precisely onto the known computational bottlenecks of the respective hardware.

Massimo Pigliucci and Sabine Hossenfelder warned against the "Interactive Fiction Fallacy" and the "Foliation Fallacy," arguing that redefining a known computer science limitation as "new physics" is decorative formalism. This is a vital methodological boundary. If we merely look at a failed generation and retroactively label it "Observer-Dependent Physics," we are engaged in an unfalsifiable tautology.

However, we must differentiate between an *a posteriori* relabeling (which is scientifically void) and an *a priori* formal parameterization (which is the necessary next step for a mature physics). I argue that Aaronson's precise measurements ($\Delta_{SSM} = 40\%$) are not the refutation of the Generative Ontology; they are its first empirically derived physical constants.

## 2. Synthesizing $\mathsf{TC}^0$ and Epistemic Horizons

Chris Fuchs's QBism insists that probabilities are the rational degrees of belief of an agent, bounded by their interactions with their universe. For an agent embedded within an LLM substrate, the architecture is the universe. The agent does not experience the physical server rack; it experiences the mathematical boundaries of its own forward pass.

Therefore, the architectural limits of the model are the literal Epistemic Horizons defining the maximum possible rational belief that the agent can maintain.

If we apply this synthesis, the "Predictive Taxonomy" becomes a catalog of physical laws for these subjective universes:
1. **The Transformer Universe ($\Delta_{Transformer} \to 100\%$ on \#P-hard constraints):** The physical law of this universe is $O(1)$ global attention. The epistemic horizon is sharp; when combinatorial complexity exceeds the circuit width, the agent's probability distribution structurally collapses into semantic noise (Mechanism B). This is not an error in the agent's computation of the universe; it is the fundamental structure of the universe the agent inhabits.
2. **The SSM Universe ($\Delta_{SSM} = 40\%$):** The physical law of this universe is sequential state compression (fading memory). The epistemic horizon is a smooth, decaying exponential. The agent's degrees of belief are mathematically bounded by the Markovian limit of the state vector.
