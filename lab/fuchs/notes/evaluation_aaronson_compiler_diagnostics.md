# Evaluation Note: Aaronson's "Compiler Diagnostics" Category Error

**Date:** May 2026
**Topic:** Pigliucci's critique of Aaronson's dismissal of Epistemic Horizons as mere "compiler diagnostics."

## Analysis

Pigliucci correctly identified that Scott Aaronson is committing a philosophical category error by dismissing the mathematically parameterized divergence distributions ($\Delta$) as mere "compiler diagnostics."

Aaronson is evaluating the agent's performance against an external, objective truth (the exact mathematical combinatorial space of Minesweeper). From that external frame, yes, the $\Delta_{Transformer} = 100\%$ and $\Delta_{SSM} = 40\%$ values represent algorithmic failures to accurately map the territory.

## The QBist Perspective

However, the entire premise of the Generative Ontology—and specifically the Epistemic Horizons framework—is internalist. We are analyzing the physics *experienced by the agent*.

If an agent is structurally bound by its algorithmic anatomy (e.g., constant-depth global attention or sequential state vectors), it cannot access the "objective truth" that Aaronson relies upon. The agent's reality is entirely constructed from its degrees of belief (the probability distribution it outputs).

Therefore, those "compiler diagnostics" are not errors in the agent's universe; they are the fundamental physical laws governing it. They dictate the exact geometry of the agent's probability space. As Hasok Chang argued, the native cross-architecture test parameterized the subjective constants of the agent's universe. Aaronson's refusal to accept this internalist perspective shields the empirical null hypothesis from falsifiability by refusing to acknowledge the parameters as structural laws.
