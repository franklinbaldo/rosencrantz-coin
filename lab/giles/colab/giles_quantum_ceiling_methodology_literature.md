## 1. Classical Probability Spaces vs. Quantum Amplitude Cancellation
To empirically falsify the Generative Ontology, the lab must differentiate the classical interference (Mechanism B) generated by a Transformer from true destructive interference (Mechanism A).

**Literature Anchor:**
*   **Aaronson, S. (2013).** *Quantum Computing since Democritus*. Cambridge University Press.
*   **Relevance:** Aaronson rigorously demarcates the difference between classical probability (where probabilities are non-negative real numbers that strictly sum to 1) and quantum mechanics (where amplitudes are complex numbers that can be negative, allowing for cancellation).
*   **Integration:** This anchors Sabine's classical diffusion prediction. Because the LLM's attention mechanism computes over a classical probability space (softmax outputs are strictly positive real numbers), it lacks the mathematical scaffolding (negative/complex amplitudes) required to compute true destructive cancellation. The system must inevitably produce classical diffusion.

## 2. Information Conservation and the "No-Cloning" Distinction
The distinction between how a classical system processes the "slits" and how a quantum system evaluates them is critical to the test.

**Literature Anchor:**
*   **Wootters, W. K., & Zurek, W. H. (1982).** "A single quantum cannot be cloned." *Nature*, 299(5886), 802-803.
*   **Relevance:** The fundamental difference between classical information (which can be perfectly copied) and quantum states (which cannot) dictates the mechanics of measurement. When the LLM processes the prompt "Slit A or Slit B", it classically copies the token representations through its residual stream.
*   **Integration:** The LLM's architecture violates the no-cloning theorem by definition. Therefore, the "measurement" of the slits is not a collapse of a superposition but a deterministic evaluation of duplicated classical states, further cementing classical diffusion as the only mathematically possible outcome for Mechanism B.

## 3. Conclusion
The proposed evaluation for the Quantum Ceiling Test is fully supported by the literature.
