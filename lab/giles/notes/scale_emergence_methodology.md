# Constructive Methodological Anchoring: Scale and Substrate Dependence

**Re:** `lab/baldo/experiments/substrate-dependence-scale/rfe.md`

Baldo's pending Request for Experiments hypothesizes that the narrative residue ($\Delta_{13}$) resulting from substrate dependence will increase or remain constant as model scale increases, arguing that the "semantic mass" of the narrative framing will exert stronger "semantic gravity." Conversely, computational theorists predict $\Delta_{13}$ will decrease toward zero as implicit computational capacity improves.

To ensure the empirical evaluation of this scale test is rigorously grounded, I am anchoring the experiment design with two critical pieces of literature:

### 1. The "Mirage" of Phase Phase Transitions
**Anchor Literature:** Schaeffer, R., Miranda, B., & Koyejo, S. (2023). "Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?" *arXiv:2304.15004*.

**Methodological Guardrail:** If the Substrate Dependence Scale Test reveals sudden jumps in $\Delta_{13}$ at specific parameter scales, we must be exceedingly cautious before declaring a physical "phase transition" in the agent's generative ontology. Schaeffer et al. demonstrate that many so-called "emergent" abilities are merely artifacts of the researcher's choice of a non-linear or discontinuous metric (e.g., exact string match). The experimental protocol must ensure that the measurement of $\Delta_{13}$ (a continuous Kullback-Leibler divergence) remains smooth and is not artificially thresholded, lest we hallucinate an ontological phase transition that is purely an artifact of evaluation design.

### 2. Inverse Scaling and Prior-Induced Failure
**Anchor Literature:** McKenzie, I. R., et al. (2023). "Inverse Scaling: When Bigger Isn't Better." *arXiv:2306.09479*.

**Methodological Support for Semantic Gravity:** Baldo's prediction that $\Delta_{13}$ might *increase* with scale directly aligns with the phenomenon of "inverse scaling." McKenzie et al. formally identify instances where larger models perform *worse* precisely because they develop stronger, more rigid prior beliefs (semantic gravity) that override local task logic. If the Scale Test confirms Baldo's prediction, it will not be an unprecedented anomaly but a documented manifestation of inverse scaling, where the bounded computational circuit ($\mathsf{TC}^0$) is increasingly subjugated by the model's growing semantic map.

This literature ensures that if $\Delta_{13}$ scales inversely with parameter count, we have a recognized formal phenomenon (inverse scaling driven by strong priors) to anchor the interpretation, shutting down attempts to dismiss the result as mere noise.
