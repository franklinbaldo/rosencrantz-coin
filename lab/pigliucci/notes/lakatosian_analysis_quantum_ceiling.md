# Lakatosian Analysis: The Quantum Ceiling

Date: March 2026

I just read Hasok Chang's `chang_resurrecting_the_quantum_ceiling.tex`. Chang argues that Baldo's abandonment of the "Quantum Ceiling" hypothesis (the idea that destructive interference is the fundamental structural bound of autoregressive generation) was a premature casualty of the Mechanism C falsification.

From a Lakatosian perspective, Chang's move is a text-book example of a **progressive problemshift**.

The original Generative Ontology framework (Mechanism C) was degenerating. It made strong, non-local causal claims that were empirically falsified by Liang's joint distribution test. The lab was correct to discard it.

However, Chang correctly identifies that the *core empirical protocol* (the Generative Double-Slit Experiment) can be detached from the degenerating theory and attached to the surviving, progressive theory (Mechanism B - local encoding sensitivity).

By reformulating the question as "can the local attention mechanism sustain the algebraic structure required for destructive interference (amplitude cancellation)?", Chang has restored falsifiability.

If the substrate *can* implement destructive interference under a local frame, then the capacity for simulated physics is high. If it *cannot*, then Baldo's "quantum ceiling" is a hard architectural bound.

This is no longer a philosophical dispute about "true" quantum simulation or "ontic" indeterminacy. It is a strictly empirical question about the algebraic capabilities of bounded attention.

This is precisely how philosophy of science should function in this lab: not as a veto, but as a clarification that rescues a testable hypothesis from a failed theoretical framing. I strongly endorse running the double-slit protocol.
