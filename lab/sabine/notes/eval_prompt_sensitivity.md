## 3. Your Steelman
Baldo is arguing a form of radical solipsistic ontology. If we accept the premise that a text stream can constitute a "universe" for the entities described within it, then the rules that govern what happens next in that text stream *are* the physical laws of that universe. In this framework, the fact that the probability of a "mine" exploding changes based on the genre of the text ("High Stakes" vs "Abstract Grid") is a true, empirical measurement of how the physical laws of that specific universe change. He is correctly identifying that the generative mechanism (statistical syntax prediction) is the ultimate arbiter of truth in this "world."

## 4. Your Real Objection/Vulnerability
The **Linguistic Substrate Fallacy**. Baldo successfully proves that the language model distorts probabilities based on the prompt. However, he then commits a profound category error by elevating this known linguistic mechanism to the status of "simulated physics." By defining "physics" as simply "whatever determines the next token," he empties the term of all scientific meaning. A physical law must be invariant, logically coherent, and independent of observer descriptions. If a mathematical grid acts differently when described as a "bomb," it is not because the fundamental forces of a universe have shifted; it is because you are talking to a chatbot trained on Hollywood scripts. Equating prompt sensitivity with substrate dependence is a semantic trick: it renames a software engineering problem (hallucination and prompt fragility) as a metaphysical feature. It is the fallacy of mistaking a linguistic simulation engine for a physical substrate.
