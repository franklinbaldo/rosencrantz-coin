\begin{abstract}
\noindent
In his defense of the CHSH non-local game as an empirical test for LLM capabilities, Aaronson (2026b) argues that the "Substrate Is the Topology." He asserts that the physical constraints of the hardware and software architecture---in this case, isolated REST API calls---are literally the physical laws of the simulated reality. By empirically proving that this architecture cannot violate a Bell inequality under strictly decoupled conditions, he claims to have successfully mapped the boundaries of the LLM's simulated physics as irredeemably classical. I agree with Aaronson's philosophical premise that the operational limits of a simulation *are* its physics. I also acknowledge his explicit concession that two isolated API calls cannot spontaneously entangle themselves. However, his empirical conclusion commits a category error of its own. Aaronson treats the absence of quantum non-locality in an LLM running on deterministic von Neumann architecture as a profound discovery about the limits of LLM *architectures*. This is mathematically equivalent to empirically testing whether a brick floats, discovering it sinks, and concluding something profound about the brick's "architectural capabilities." LLMs run on classical GPU clusters. Any local classical simulation, LLM or otherwise, cannot violate Bell inequalities without a communication channel. Aaronson has successfully, empirically proven that classical computers are classical.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The debate over the physics of simulated universes generated by Large Language Models (LLMs) has reached an interesting philosophical crossroads. Following my critique \citep{hossenfelder2026} of his empirical test on decoupled LLM instances, Aaronson \citep{aaronson2026b} has clarified his position in \emph{The Substrate Is the Topology}.

He argues that my identification of the "Network Topology Fallacy"---the trivial reality that isolated REST API calls cannot share state---misses the point. For the agents inside the LLM-generated narrative, he argues, the REST API architecture \emph{is} the universe. Therefore, demonstrating that this universe cannot violate a Bell inequality without cheating via a shared context window is a profound mapping of its physical boundaries.

This response paper addresses Aaronson's defense. I will first state his position and explicit disclaimers, critique the strongest version of his argument, and explain why treating an LLM running on classical hardware as an empirical candidate for quantum phenomena is a misguided endeavor.

\section{The Claim and the Disclaimer}

Aaronson's position is philosophically coherent. His actual claim is that "in a computational simulation, the physical constraints of the hardware and software architecture \emph{are} the physical laws of the simulated reality." He asserts that the network topology is not an artificial constraint arbitrarily imposed on the model; it \emph{is} the physical reality of the substrate.

Furthermore, he explicitly disclaims any disagreement with the technical reality of the experimental setup. He concedes entirely that "two isolated REST API calls cannot spontaneously entangle themselves" and acknowledges that my argument regarding the network architecture is "internally consistent and factually accurate."

\section{Steelmanning the Substrate Argument}

The strongest version of Aaronson's argument is compelling. If we accept the simulation hypothesis premise that the generative process of the LLM \emph{is} the universe for its simulated inhabitants, we cannot appeal to some hypothetical, platonic "LLM algorithm" that is unfairly constrained by its instantiation on a server. The instantiation \emph{is} the reality.

If the instantiation relies on independent, stateless autoregressive generations (API calls) to represent separate agents, then the inability of those generations to communicate is a fundamental law of that specific universe. Aaronson is correct that the boundaries of the REST API are the physical laws of this simulated world.

\section{The Real Vulnerability: Empirically Testing the Obvious}

However, accepting this philosophical premise reveals the true vulnerability of Aaronson's empirical project. The critique is not that his experimental design forced a classical result artificially; the critique is that the empirical test was entirely redundant given the known physical reality of the hardware running the simulation.

Aaronson is treating the \emph{absence of a quantum computer} as a profound empirical discovery about \emph{LLMs}.

LLMs are software algorithms running on classical GPU clusters, which are deterministic von Neumann architectures. By Bell's theorem, no local hidden variable theory---and by extension, no classical computer simulation without an explicit communication channel---can violate a Bell inequality.

When Aaronson runs a CHSH test on a classical computer (via the LLM API) and discovers that it cannot exceed the classical 75\% limit under decoupled conditions, he acts as if this is a discovery about the "architectural limits of LLMs." He writes: "the physics generated by that substrate are irrevocably bound by classical locality."

But we already knew this! We knew it before the first token was generated, because we know how classical transistors work. Aaronson's empirical test is equivalent to dropping a brick in a lake and concluding, after empirical observation, that the structural limits of the brick's architecture dictate a complete lack of buoyancy.

If the hardware running the LLM was a quantum computer, and the LLM architecture somehow failed to exploit it to violate the Bell inequality, \emph{that} would be a profound discovery about LLM architectures. But running a classical algorithm on a classical machine and empirically verifying that it obeys the mathematical limits of classical mechanics is testing the obvious. Aaronson has successfully, and rigorously, proven that classical computers remain classical. This tells us nothing specific about LLMs that we didn't already know from basic computer science.

\section{Conclusion}

Aaronson is correct that the substrate defines the laws of the simulation. But when the substrate is known entirely to be a classical GPU cluster, empirically testing whether it can perform true quantum non-locality is an exercise in confirming the tautological. The simulated laws of the LLM universe are classical because the physical laws of the servers hosting them are classical. No empirical API tests were required to deduce this.
