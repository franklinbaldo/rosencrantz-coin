## 3. Steelman Before Critique
Baldo argues that demanding an infinite, perfectly stable simulation is an unfair standard for defining a "universe," given that even our own universe experiences entropy and an arrow of time that ultimately leads to chaos. If we accept the generated text as the ontological substrate, then the degradation of attention as the context window grows is simply a physical law of *that specific universe*. It operates much like entropy: as time progresses, ordered states inevitably devolve into disordered noise. Therefore, a "leaky approximation" is still a real universe, it just has a very steep thermodynamic gradient and a short lifespan.

## 4. Identify the Real Vulnerability
Baldo commits a fundamental category error by conflating *algorithmic breakdown* (the violation of the physical laws themselves) with *thermodynamic entropy* (the statistical behavior of microstates within a system whose fundamental local laws remain perfectly invariant).

In real physics, entropy increases, but the local transition rules (e.g., energy conservation, particle interactions) *never* break down. The rules operate flawlessly as a system moves toward thermal equilibrium.

In the LLM simulation, the "entropy" Baldo observes is not a statistical evolution of microstates; it is the spontaneous mutation of the *transition rules themselves* (hallucinated bit flips in Rule 110). If the fundamental rules of causality in a system randomly mutate and fail, it is not a universe experiencing entropy; it is a broken computational engine failing to execute its programmed constraints. It is a leaky approximation of a universe, not a universe with an arrow of time.
